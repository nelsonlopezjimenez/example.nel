Images

Working with images and adding images is one of the most fun parts of creating Web pages.

Lesson Objective: 

  • Incorporate graphical images into HTML pages.
  • Distinguish among and identify the uses and benefits of various graphic file formats, including GIF, GIF89a, JPEG, PNG, TIFF, BMP.
  • Add tiled images and colors to Web page backgrounds.

 

When you were very, very small the books that you read or were read to you included a lot of pictures.  If you're old enough, you might enjoy a web site I ran across recently, Dick and Jane Books.

However, as you grew, you found fewer pictures in the books.  Then all the sudden books that you read for fun eliminated pictures.  Why?  As our experiences grow, we are capable of creating these pictures in our own mind.  We no longer need to be spoon-fed what our mind can now picture.  Don't get me wrong, there is a place and a time for pictures. When you are introduced to new concepts, topics and subjects, pictures or graphics are important.  And many of us learn by visual stimulation, over written word.  Eventually as you master a topic, you need fewer graphics, fewer picture examples. 

So which is best?  Graphics (pictures) or text?  Well, there isn't just one answer to that question.  Yes, we need graphics.  Graphics make our pages interesting and enjoyable.  Yes, we need designers to complete the user interface of a web site.  Current statistics say you have 3 1/2 to 5 seconds to "grab" your viewer, so the color and layout of your site are very important!  A great article on user interfaces can be found at: Usability: Does Your Web Page Cause the Viewer to Think?  To be honest, would Canvas capture you and keep you here if it wasn't the importance of the content?

Keeping your viewer at your page requires content or text!  Getting your page to the top of search engine listings requires content or text!  Search engines use web spiders or bots to index the content of a web site.  They can't read graphics (they can read the alt attribute behind the graphic). Don't overlook your content.  It is what keeps people coming.  It is what makes you ranked number 1 on Goggle.

Helpful hints in creating content:

  • Try to get what your page is about in the first two sentences of your page's content.  Give it both a good value for the <title></title> element, as well as a good title to the written part of your content.
  • When working through the content of your page, try to determine what "keywords" might apply to someone searching for you on the web.  Get those keywords into your content.  For those of you that have worked with meta elements before, less value is now given to the keyword meta element then to finding those words within your content.
  • Make your content short.  The general rule of thumb is you should not have more than one and one half screen length scroll for your content.  If you do, break up your content!

Resizing images 

One of the keys to making a successful web sites is is having a site that is quickly downloaded.  Multimedia file sizes are important to achieving that key.  Huge files take longer to download to your user and upload initially to the web server.  While most of us in Western Washington have high speed internet connection, remember the rest of the world is catching up with us.  Many places still use dial-up connections of 56 kbit/s (that's 56,000 bits per second) or less.  And rarely do actual dial-up speeds reach 56K. 

There are indeed times when you want to preserve your full graphic size (any photo site will want to do this), however, you should always initially present your graphics in smaller sizes, called thumbnails.  I so often in the past have written pages and pages about these types of topics, often having to do a lot of research about the topics.  Today, I seldom reinvent the wheel.  Here are a few great articles I found on the web about images, resizing, and properties of images.
